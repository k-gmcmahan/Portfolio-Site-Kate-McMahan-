Schnitzeljagd

The Problem

Scavenger hunts can be time-consuming to set-up, coordinate, and judge. Timing is also a vital element. Teams cannot afford to waste it by flipping through pages of poorly organized lists or navigating clunky interfaces.

The Solution

This app should have a highly intuitive interface that doesn’t force the user to waste time endlessly scrolling or jumping around screens. It should have a robust messaging platform that allows communication on multiple levels (ex. moderator to participants, teammate to teammate, etc.). Finally, the app should do whatever it can to alleviate the workload for the user--whether that be through pre-loaded challenges, accurate live updates, or automatically organized/calculated challenge submissions.

The Approach

Observations and Insights

In this early stage, I researched the scavenger hunting apps that are currently available--comparing their features, pricing, layouts, and overall style. I also conducted user interviews to meet users face-to-face and learn about their experiences with scavenger hunting and social gaming apps. Competitive research analysis and user interviews both helped me see what user needs the market has not yet met--seamlessly integrated social features with an intuitive interface.

Conceptualization

User personas were created based on data gathered from research and interviews.

From this stage, the information architecture for Schnitzeljagd began to take shape through user journeys and user flows.

The app’s preliminary framework was finally put to the test through card sorting. This helped me rework the sitemap that the prototype would be based on.

Iteration

With a basic structure in place, it was time to bring the prototype to life! The wireframes began with rough sketches on pencil and paper. From there, I continued to make adjustments and add details until I had a testable prototype.

Due to the divided opinions on how the app’s landing and onboarding screens were presented, I decided that this would be a good area to conduct a preference test.

Using these new sets of data, I had enough knowledge to start making improvements to the prototype! I also paid close attention to incorporating more Gestalt Properties and Visual Design Principles. A style guide was compiled from the improved prototype.

Takeaways

What went well:

  • Thorough competitor research and user interviews really paid off and gave me a solid sense of direction. These gave me three persona’s with unique needs that I was able to address while prototyping.
  • Card sorting also did a great job of forcing me to step back from the project. As a result, I caught a few glaring mistakes in the information architecture of Schnitzeljagd before prototyping began.
  • Although I needed to tweak some button designs and colors, the overall layout and style of Schnitzeljagd was well-recieved.

What can be improved:

During this project I was better familiarizing myself with a wider range of design software. While this was an excellent learning experience, effeciency became an issue towards the late design stages. I have since learned a variety software command shortcuts and added to my library of personal assets. This should help my future projects along much faster while creating and adjusting prototypes.
